01 — The Problem

Legacy Software vs. a Room Full of Kids

School libraries run on a kind of beautiful chaos. Picture thirty kids descending on you at once, everyone wanting something different, and roughly forty-five seconds per checkout before someone knocks a display stand over. That's the actual working environment.

The software the librarian was using wasn't built for any of that. It was built for someone sitting quietly at a desk, with time to navigate nested menus and type in ISBN numbers by hand. Calm, considered, sequential. Everything a busy school library is not.

In practice, she'd quietly stopped using it for daily checkouts. She was keeping a paper notebook.

That told me everything I needed to know about where to start.

02 — The Solution

Scan First, Think Later

The whole thing came down to one idea: make the scan happen first, and let everything else follow from that. No menus to dig through before you know what book you're dealing with. No decisions to make before the app knows what it's looking at.

Point. Scan. Act. That's it.

The three states cover virtually every real-world scenario. Unknown book — the app flags it immediately and asks if you want to add it. Available — it drops you straight into checkout. Already on loan — it tells you who has it and gives you a one-tap return. No dead ends. No "what do I do now?"

03 — How I Got There

Reality-Checking the Prototype

The first prototype looked great in Figma. Elegant, considered, nicely spaced. Then I handed it to the librarian and watched what happened.

She picked up the phone, pointed it at a book, and immediately asked "so... what do I press?" The confirmation button I'd designed was sitting quietly in the bottom-right corner. Tasteful. Completely invisible when you're distracted and surrounded by noise.

That was the moment I stopped designing for calm and started designing for chaos.

Everything got bigger. Confirmations became full-screen states instead of small overlays. Buttons expanded until they were impossible to miss. Colour contrast went up until it had headroom to spare above accessibility thresholds.

The question I kept coming back to: if someone's half-distracted and a bit rushed, is this still obvious? If the answer was no, it wasn't done yet.
